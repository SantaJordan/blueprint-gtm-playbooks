Blueprint GTM Playbook for Blockit

Who the Hell is Jordan Crawford?

Founder of Blueprint GTM. Built a business by scraping 25M+ job posts to find company pain points. Believes the Predictable Revenue model is dead. Thinks mounting an AI SDR on outdated methodology is like putting a legless robot on a horse—no one gets anywhere, and it still shits along the way.

The core philosophy is simple: The message isn't the problem. The LIST is the message. When you know exactly who to target and why they need you right now, the message writes itself.

The Old Way (What Everyone Does)

Let's be brutally honest about what your GTM team is doing right now. They're buying lists from ZoomInfo, adding some "personalization" like mentioning a LinkedIn post, then blasting generic messages about features. Here's what it actually looks like:

The Typical Blockit SDR Email:

Subject: Quick Question about Patient Access Hi [First Name], I noticed on LinkedIn that [Hospital Name] recently expanded your service lines. Congrats on the growth! I wanted to reach out because we work with health systems like Houston Methodist and others to help with patient scheduling and referral management. Our platform provides real-time scheduling, reduces call center volume by 40%, and improves patient satisfaction. We've helped systems reduce patient leakage by up to 25%. Would you have 15 minutes next week to explore how we might be able to help [Hospital Name]? Best, Generic SDR

Why this fails: The prospect is a Director of Patient Access who's dealt with scheduling vendors for years. They've seen this template 1,000 times. There's zero indication you understand their specific HCAHPS scores, their merger integration challenges, or their actual patient access metrics. It's interruption disguised as personalization. Delete.

The New Way: Intelligence-Driven GTM

Blueprint GTM flips the entire approach. Instead of interrupting prospects with pitches, you deliver insights so valuable they'd pay consulting fees to receive them. You become the person who helps them see around corners, not another vendor in their inbox.

This requires two fundamental shifts:

1. Hard Data Over Soft Signals

Stop: "I see you're expanding your service lines" (press release - everyone sees this)

Start: "Your HCAHPS responsiveness score is 54%—13 points below the 67% national benchmark" (CMS government data with exact percentages)

2. Mirror Situations, Don't Pitch Solutions

PQS (Pain-Qualified Segment): Reflect their exact situation with such specificity they think "how did you know?" Use CMS data with specific scores, merger timelines, facility counts.

Strong PQS: Combine government data with competitive intelligence to show them something non-obvious about their market position or integration challenges.

Blockit PQS Plays: Mirroring Exact Situations

These messages demonstrate such precise understanding of the prospect's current situation that they feel genuinely seen. Every claim traces to CMS data or public merger announcements.

PQS 7.8/10

Play 1: HCAHPS Responsiveness Gap

What's the play? Target hospitals with 4-5 star overall ratings but below-average HCAHPS "staff responsiveness" scores. This pattern indicates excellent clinical care but poor patient access—exactly the problem Blockit solves. The disconnect between overall quality and responsiveness creates internal tension that scheduling technology can address.

Why this works: The buyer knows their HCAHPS scores but may not have framed the "clinical quality vs. operational access" gap this way. By connecting their responsiveness score to referral leakage risk, you're offering a new lens on familiar data. The question "Is that what you're seeing internally?" requires zero effort to answer and opens conversation.

Data source: CMS HCAHPS Patient Survey (data.cms.gov/provider-data/dataset/dgck-syfz) | Fields: H_RESP_HSP_A_P (responsiveness %), Hospital overall rating | Confidence: 95%

The message:

Subject: 54% staff responsiveness Your facility's HCAHPS reports 54% of patients said staff "always" responded quickly—13 points below the 67% national benchmark. Interestingly, your overall star rating is 4 stars, suggesting strong clinical care but an access bottleneck. The gap usually lives in scheduling coordination: patients waiting on hold, referrals falling through cracks, staff manually checking calendars across departments. Is that what you're seeing internally?
PQS 8.0/10

Play 2: Post-Merger Integration Gap

What's the play? Target recently merged health systems (6-18 months post-close) with the predictable scheduling integration challenge. Merged systems must coordinate scheduling across multiple EMRs, and this creates a known pain window. Use public merger announcements to time outreach precisely.

Why this works: The buyer is living this pain daily—referrals disappearing between legacy systems, patients unable to book across the full network. By naming the specific merger, hospital count, and timeline, you demonstrate you've done homework. The Houston Methodist case study provides proof that the problem is solvable.

Data source: Public merger announcements (Becker's Hospital Review, Chief Healthcare Executive) + CMS Hospital General Information | Confidence: 95%

The message:

Subject: 32-hospital integration Jefferson Health completed the Lehigh Valley merger in August 2024—32 hospitals, multiple EMRs, 6 months into integration. The typical pattern: scheduling systems that don't talk to each other, referrals that disappear between legacy platforms, and patients who can't book across the full network. Houston Methodist solved this exact problem by building a unified scheduling layer across their multi-EMR environment. Is cross-system scheduling coordination on your integration roadmap, or still in triage?

Blockit Strong PQS Plays: Competitive Intelligence

These messages combine government data with calculated insights to show prospects something non-obvious about their market position. They offer value in exchange for engagement.

STRONG PQS 7.6/10

Play 1: Regional HCAHPS Responsiveness Ranking

What's the play? Calculate the prospect's HCAHPS responsiveness ranking vs. all acute care hospitals in their metro area. Show them where they stand competitively—information they don't have easy access to. Offer to send the full ranking as a value exchange.

Why this works: Patient Access Directors track their own scores but rarely benchmark against local competitors. Seeing "you're #38 of 47" creates competitive urgency. The offer to send the full ranking is a low-friction next step that delivers genuine value.

Data source: CMS HCAHPS + Hospital General Information (calculated ranking) | Fields: H_RESP_HSP_A_P, Facility location | Confidence: 75% (calculated inference on government data)

The message:

Subject: Your responsiveness benchmark I pulled CMS HCAHPS data for the 47 acute care hospitals in your metro area and ranked them by "staff responsiveness" scores. Your facility ranks #38 of 47—bottom quartile for patient-reported wait times. Here's the interesting part: the top 5 hospitals average 4.2 minutes to first response in their patient surveys. Your score suggests ~8.5 minutes. That 4-minute gap is where referrals leak to competitors. I'll send the full metro ranking if useful. Should this go to you or your patient experience lead?
STRONG PQS 7.6/10

Play 2: Post-Merger Integration Architecture

What's the play? For recently merged systems, offer to walk through how a comparable health system (Houston Methodist) solved the multi-EMR scheduling challenge. This positions you as a consultant, not a vendor, and the case study becomes your proof point.

Why this works: Integration leaders are hungry for playbooks from systems that have solved their exact problem. By offering architecture insights rather than a product demo, you lower the barrier to engagement. The question "Who's leading scheduling integration?" routes you to the right decision-maker.

Data source: CMS Hospital General Information (facility counts) + Blockit case study (Houston Methodist) | Confidence: 85%

The message:

Subject: Integration blind spot I reviewed CMS facility data for your newly merged system—32 hospitals across two legacy networks. Based on typical multi-EMR environments, there are likely 3-5 scheduling platforms that don't share real-time availability with each other. Houston Methodist tackled this same challenge—6 hospitals, 1,890 physicians, multiple EMR systems—and built a unified scheduling layer that gives patients and staff access to network-wide availability in real-time. I can walk through their architecture if that's relevant to your integration timeline. Who's leading scheduling integration on your side?

The Transformation

Notice the difference? Traditional outreach talks about YOUR product and YOUR benefits. Blueprint GTM talks about THEIR situation and THEIR challenges using verifiable CMS data they can look up themselves.

The shift is simple but profound:

Stop sending messages about what you do. Start sending intelligence about what they need to know right now. When you lead with "54% staff responsiveness—13 points below the 67% national benchmark" instead of "we help with patient scheduling," you're not another sales email—you're the person who actually pulled their HCAHPS data.

This isn't about templates or tactics. It's about building a systematic way to identify hospitals experiencing specific, urgent challenges where Blockit's multi-EMR scheduling platform provides unique value—and proving you've done the homework with CMS data and merger timelines.

The companies that master this approach don't compete on features. They compete on intelligence.
